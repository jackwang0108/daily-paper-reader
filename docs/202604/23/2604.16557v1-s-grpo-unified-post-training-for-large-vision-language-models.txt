SFT forces the model’s generation along a single, deterministic expert trajectory, often inducing catastrophic forgetting of general multimodal capa-bilities due to distributional shifts. ## 1 Introduction 

Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) [ 2–4 , 7, 8 , 16 , 26 , 27 , 31 , 39 –41 , 53 ] have demonstrated remarkable general-purpose capabilities by unifying visual perception with natural language understanding. Recently, the focus of alignment research has shifted towards adapt-ing these foundational models to highly specialized domains, such as medical image diagnostics, complex geometric problem-solving, and fine-grained industrial visual inspection. In these scenarios, the model must not only perceive visual tokens but also adhere to rigor-ous logical constraints and domain-specific rubrics. To achieve this domain adaptation, prevailing post-training techniques typically fall into two distinct categories: Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT-like methods) [ 17 , 22 , 29 ] and Reinforcement Learning (RL-like meth-ods) [11, 35, 42–44, 46–49]. Although both paradigms are widely adopted, as shown in Fig-ure 1, each suffers from profound structural limitations resulting from how they manage the autoregressive generation process. SFT [ 29 ] utilizes maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) to force the model along a single, deterministic ground-truth trajectory . While highly efficient for injecting specific domain knowledge, this heavy reliance on strict teacher-forcing introduces a significant distribu-tional shift. This shift often triggers the "Alignment Tax" [ 25 ]—a form of catastrophic forgetting where the model’s broad, general-purpose multimodal competencies are aggressively overwritten by the narrow target distribution. In stark contrast, RL algorithms—such as Group Relative Policy Op-timization [ 35 ] (GRPO)—attempt to refine the policy by sampling 

multiple diverse trajectories and optimizing them against a reward function. However, directly applying RL to an unaligned base model exposes the severe "cold-start" problem in exploration [ 42 ]. In spe-cialized visual domains evaluated by strict binary verifiers, the base model struggles to spontaneously generate highly accurate trajec-tories. When using group-based RL, if all sampled trajectories in a group are incorrect, the reward variance collapses to zero, leading to vanishing gradients and stalled learning. Consequently, prac-titioners face a fundamental dilemma: SFT limits optimization to a single trajectory causing catastrophic forgetting, while pure RL explores multiple trajectories but suffers from optimization collapse due to reward sparsity. We argue that the dichotomy between SFT and RL is artificial. Mathematically, SFT can be viewed as an RL process where the singular ground-truth trajectory is exclusively assigned a maximal reward. This perspective suggests that the transition from super-vised learning to reinforcement learning should be a continuous spectrum rather than a disjointed sequence of stages. If we could integrate this strong, unambiguous supervision signal directly into the multi-trajectory online exploration phase of RL, we could theo-retically synthesize the strengths of both paradigms. By treating the ground-truth trajectory as an active participant in the pref-erence comparison process—an "anchor" with a guaranteed high reward—we can leverage the contrastive nature of policy gradients to seamlessly unify the definitive expert imitation of SFT with the autonomous multi-trajectory self-exploration of RL within a single optimization landscape. Based on this insight, we propose Supervised Group Relative Policy Optimization (S-GRPO), a unified post-training framework tailored for LVLMs. S-GRPO introduces a dynamic mechanism: Conditional Ground-Truth Trajectory Injection (CGI) . During online sampling, if a simple binary verifier detects that the policy has failed to gen-erate any valid trajectories within an exploratory group, S-GRPO conditionally injects the verified ground-truth trajectory into the candidate pool. The relative advantage is then computed against this mixed group. The injected trajectory acts as a high-advantage anchor, guiding the gradient steeply toward the correct multimodal distribution. As the model improves and begins generating correct trajectories autonomously, the CGI mechanism gracefully phases out, allowing training to seamlessly transition from supervised bootstrapping to pure self-exploratory reinforcement learning. Our main contributions are summarized as follows: 

• Unified Post-Training Framework : We formalize S-GRPO, an algorithm that elegantly integrates the objectives of SFT and preference optimization, eliminating the need to choose between isolated paradigms and mitigating catastrophic forget-ting in LVLMs. 

• Mechanism Design : We propose the Conditional Ground-Truth Trajectory Injection (CGI) strategy, which dynamically solves the exploration cold-start problem in binary-verifier settings by guaranteeing positive learning signals within multi-trajectory updates. 

• Empirical Performance : We demonstrate that S-GRPO signifi-cantly accelerates convergence in sparse-reward visual domains and achieves superior domain-specific performance while pre-serving broad general capabilities compared to other methods. 

## 2 Related Works 2.1 Large Vision Language Models (LVLMs) 

Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) [ 2–4 , 7, 8 , 16 , 26 , 27 , 31 , 36 ,39 –41 , 53 ] have driven remarkable advancements in cross-modal intelligence. This evolution unfolded across three key phases: early foundational architectures (e.g., CLIP [ 32 ], ALBEF [ 21 ]) bridged the modality gap for zero-shot transfer; subsequent models like LLaVA [ 28 ] elevated general capabilities via visual instruction tuning; and current state-of-the-art models—such as the InternVL series [ 40 ,41 , 53 ], Qwen-VL family [ 2– 4 , 31 , 39 ], and Kimi K2.5 [ 36 ], push the boundaries through massive scaling, high-resolution encoders, and native long-context processing. Despite these extraordinary general-purpose capabilities, LVLMs still face significant hurdles in domain-specific adaptation. When deployed in highly specialized environments (e.g., medical diagnostics), they frequently struggle to recognize domain-exclusive concepts. Consequently, post-training has emerged as a critical research direction to adapt these massive LVLMs efficiently without the prohibitive computational costs of retraining from scratch. ## 2.2 Post-training for LVLMs 

Post-training techniques, primarily Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning (RL), have been central to adapting LVLMs [ 9 , 19 , 20 , 23 ]. SFT enables direct task-specific learning but often leads to catastrophic forgetting of pre-trained knowledge [5 , 12 , 13 ]. While parameter-efficient approaches like LoRA [ 17 ] and Prefix Tuning [ 22 ] alleviate computational burdens, they remain prone to overfitting and exhibit limited transferability. In contrast, RL-based methods enhance adaptability by optimiz-ing sequential decision-making. Unlike traditional Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) [ 34 ], which imposes high computational costs, recent alternatives like Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [ 33 ]and Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) [ 35 ] eliminate the need for a separate critic. While advanced frameworks (e.g., DAPO [46 ], GSPO [ 49 ]) effectively preserve general capabilities by explor-ing diverse trajectories, they implicitly assume that the pre-trained model already possesses non-trivial domain knowledge. When do-main expertise is limited, existing RL paradigms inevitably suffer from reward sparsity and optimization collapse. The commonly adopted “SFT-then-RL” pipeline [ 35 ] attempts to alleviate this, but recent findings from CHORD [ 47 ] reveal a fundamental flaw: SFT disrupts the pretrained model’s internal structures, causing tempo-rary degradation of general capabilities, and subsequent RL often fails to recover the domain adaptation. To mitigate optimization collapse, recent studies have explored ad-vanced RL strategies. Curriculum-based approaches (e.g., R3 [ 43 ], Curr-ReFT [ 11 ]) attempt to smooth the learning curve for complex tasks. To address the “learning cliff” where models fail to gener-ate correct trajectories, methods like GCPO [ 42 ] and Scaf-GRPO [ 48 ] intervene by injecting golden answers or hierarchical hints. Other works focus on modifying the RL objective; for instance, FAST-GRPO [ 44 ] mitigates the “overthinking” phenomenon via difficulty-aware length penalties. While demonstrating remarkable efficacy, these methods often rely on complex decoupled multi-stage pipelines, external heavy teacher models, or are exclusively tailored for multi-step reasoning (Chain-of-Thought) tasks. Addressing the specific challenge of optimization collapse in non-deep-thinking vision tasks, prior work introduced Reinforced Cur-riculum Pre-Alignment (RCPA) [ 45 ]. By utilizing Curriculum Progress Perception (CPP) for intrinsic prefix-injection, RCPA effectively bootstraps domain competence directly within the on-policy RL loop. However, RCPA still necessitates a carefully designed curricu-lum schedule and relies on explicitly manipulating the generation prefix, which adds algorithmic complexity and restricts the model’s free exploration. Building upon the insights of RCPA, our proposed S-GRPO frame-work offers a more elegant and genuinely unified single-stage so-lution. Instead of relying on predefined curriculum progression or prefix-injection, S-GRPO utilizes Conditional Ground-Truth Tra-jectory Injection (CGI) dynamically triggered by group-level ex-ploratory failure. By anchoring the policy gradient with verified expert trajectories only when strictly necessary, S-GRPO natively resolves extreme reward sparsity. This gracefully bridges the SFT and RL paradigms without the overhead of curriculum design, max-imizing sample efficiency while preserving the structural integrity and general capabilities of the base LVLM.

## 3.3 The Cold-Start Challenge and Optimization Collapse 

While GRPO effectively explores multiple trajectories, it heavily relies on the assumption that the unaligned base policy can sponta-neously generate at least some correct trajectories within a group to establish a meaningful contrastive gradient. However, in specialized visual domains evaluated by strict verifiers, the base model suffers from a severe cold-start problem . The proba-bility of spontaneously generating a valid response is infinitesimally small. Consequently, all 𝐺 sampled trajectories are likely to fail, re-sulting in uniformly zero or negative rewards (𝑒.𝑔., 𝑟 𝑖 = 0 for all 𝑖 ).When this occurs, the group statistics collapse: mean ({ 𝑟 𝑖 }) ≈ 0, std ({ 𝑟 𝑖 }) ≈ 0

According to Eq. (2), this leads to uninformative, near-zero advan-tage estimates ( ˆ𝐴 𝑖 ≈ 0). The contrastive signals vanish, causing an 

optimization collapse where learning completely stalls. This funda-mental limitation motivates our proposed unified approach, which actively injects supervisory signals into the RL exploration loop to dynamically break the optimization deadlock.

## 4.3 Conditional Ground-Truth Trajectory Injection (CGI) 

While baseline injection seamlessly mitigates the cold-start problem, unconditionally injecting the ground-truth trajectory throughout the entire training process may inadvertently reintroduce the strict teacher-forcing bias prevalent in SFT. To balance the exploration-exploitation trade-off, we propose a refined variant: Conditional Ground-Truth Trajectory Injection (CGI) .CGI employs the ground-truth trajectory 𝑦 𝑔𝑡 strictly as a dynamic fallback mechanism, triggered exclusively when the policy experi-ences a complete exploratory failure. For a query 𝑥 , the process is as follows: (1) Initial Exploration: We sample a full group of 𝐺 candidate tra-jectories strictly from the current policy: 𝑂 𝑔𝑒𝑛 = {𝑜 1, . . . , 𝑜 𝐺 } ∼ 

𝜋 𝜃 𝑜𝑙𝑑 (·| 𝑥 ).(2) Verification and Failure Detection: The verifier evaluates all 

𝐺 trajectories to assign rewards {𝑟 1, . . . , 𝑟 𝐺 }. We define a group failure condition as the scenario where all generated trajectories fail to yield a positive reward (e.g., max ({ 𝑟 1, . . . , 𝑟 𝐺 }) = 0). (3) Conditional Fallback: 

• Condition Met (Complete Failure): If triggered, the model is trapped in a cold-start state. We discard one generated trajec-tory (typically the one with the lowest generation probability) and replace it with 𝑦 𝑔𝑡 , forming the mixed group 𝑂 𝑚𝑖𝑥𝑒𝑑 . The policy update proceeds using the unified S-GRPO formula-tion (Equation 7). 

• Condition Not Met (Successful Exploration): If at least one gen-erated trajectory receives a positive reward from the verifier (𝑟 > 0), the model possesses sufficient capability to learn from its own exploratory variance. No injection occurs, and the policy is updated using the standard GRPO objective (Equation 3) over the original set 𝑂 𝑔𝑒𝑛 .Unlike GCPO’s unconditional injection (which introduces SFT-like teacher-forcing bias) and RCPA’s curriculum-dependent prefix in-jection (which requires manual design), S-GRPO’s CGI mechanism dynamically triggers ground-truth injection only when necessary (complete exploration failure). This design achieves a seamless transition from supervised bootstrapping to pure reinforcement learning in a single stage, eliminating external dependencies (e.g., curriculum modules) while balancing exploration and exploitation. 

## 5 Experiments 

In this section, we empirically validate the effectiveness of S-GRPO on Large Vision-Language Models. Our experiments are designed to address three key questions: (1) Can S-GRPO successfully train a visual generation policy from a "cold-start" state where a strict bi-nary verifier causes standard RL methods to collapse? (2) How does the unified single-stage S-GRPO compare against the prevalent two-stage pipeline (SFT followed by GRPO) in terms of domain-specific Conference XXX, 2026, Yan et al. 

performance and general capability retention? (3) What is the im-pact of the Conditional Ground-Truth Injection (CGI) mechanism on learning dynamics? 

## 5.1 Experimental Setup 

Benchmark Datasets. To evaluate S-GRPO’s adaptability and performance, we conduct experiments on three benchmark datasets spanning diverse domains: image captioning, geometric problem-solving, and medical X-ray diagnostics. 

• COCO Caption [ 6]: A widely used dataset for image captioning, containing 123,287 images. We use the original training and test splits of the dataset. 

• Geo170K [ 15 ]: A dataset for geometric problem-solving. It is divided into Phase 1 (non-deep thinking, direct-answer tasks) and Phase 2 (deep thinking, multi-step tasks). We use Phase 1, which contains 60,252 samples, with 57,252 for training and 3,000 for testing. 

• OpenI [ 10 ]: A chest X-ray diagnostic dataset with 6,423 images and corresponding radiological reports. The task is to generate concise and clinically accurate diagnostic descriptions directly from X-ray images. The training set consists of 5,423 images, while the test set includes 1,000 images. These datasets enable us to evaluate S-GRPO’s performance across both general domain (e.g., COCO Caption) and specific domain (e.g., Geo170K, OpenI) tasks, providing a comprehensive assessment of domain-adaptive capabilities. 

Baselines. We employ Qwen2.5-VL-7B [ 4 ] as our primary base model. The selection of this specific model is deliberate: it possesses foundational multimodal capabilities without being heavily over-optimized for existing benchmarks, and crucially, it has not been pre-aligned using GRPO or similar advanced RL paradigms. This ensures an unbiased testbed, allowing the genuine effectiveness and performance gains of our proposed framework to be unequivocally demonstrated. We compare the following adaptation strategies: (1) BASE : Direct inference with the pre-trained Qwen2.5-VL-7B. (2) SFT-based Methods : Including Parameter-Efficient Fine-tuning (PEFT) via LoRA [ 17 ] and Full Fine-tuning (FFT). Furthermore, drawing on relevant methods in incremental learning [ 18 ], we incorporate the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence loss into Full Fine-Tuning (FFT), and define this improved approach as Continual Full Fine-Tuning (CFFT). Specifically, this KL divergence loss imposes a constraint that aligns the output distribution of the fine-tuned model with that of the pre-trained model, effectively mitigating the overwriting of general capabilities by domain-specific signals. (3) RL-based Methods : Including GRPO [ 35 ], DAPO [ 46 ], GR-PON [ 45 ], GCPO [ 42 ], and RCPA [ 45 ]. To ensure a rigorous and equitable evaluation, all RL-based methods, including our S-GRPO, are evaluated in a strictly RL-driven setting without relying on any external "SFT-then-RL" pre-conditioning. For completeness, an empirical comparison of the conventional two-stage "SFT-then-RL" pipeline (SFT → GRPON) is provided in Table 1. Furthermore, to ensure a strictly fair and apples-to-apples com-parison, we deliberately exclude approaches that rely on external auxiliary models or proprietary LLMs for prompt generation and knowledge distillation (e.g., Curr-ReFT [ 11 ] and Scaf-GRPO [ 48 ]). Including such methods would obscure the intrinsic effectiveness of the alignment algorithms, as their performance gains are heavily confounded by the capabilities of the external models rather than the alignment process itself. 

Evaluation Metrics. We evaluate S-GRPO using a combination of task-specific metrics for domain knowledge injection and gen-eralization metrics for preserving general-purpose performance. Domain-specific ability is assessed with BLEU-1 [30 ], CIDEr [ 38 ], 

ROUGE-L [ 24 ], and SPICE [ 1 ] to measure n-gram overlap, seman-tic consistency, long-sequence similarity, and structural alignment. General-purpose capabilities are evaluated using MMMU [14 ] for cross-domain reasoning, MME [ 14 ] for multimodal understanding, and IFEval [52 ]—including IFEval-Prompt (IFEval-P) and IFEval-Instruct (IFEval-I)—to measure instruction-following fidelity and consistency with human intent. 

Implementation Details. For model training and inference, we set the hyperparameters 𝛿 in Equation 4 to 0.9, the regularization coeffi-cient 𝛽 in Equation 3 is set to 0.01. For group-based methods, we set the group size 𝐺 = 5. We employ a singular, robust binary verifier (e.g., Qwen3-4B-Instruct Verifier) that returns 1.0 for correct direct responses and 0 otherwise. All experiments are implemented using the EasyR1 RL framework [ 50 ] and LlamaFactory SFT framework [51 ] for LVLMs and conducted on a Linux-based server equipped with NVIDIA GPUs.

## 5.3 Ablation Study 

Effectiveness of Conditional Injection. To validate the core innovation of S-GRPO, we ablate the Ground-Truth Injection mech-anism on the COCO Caption dataset. As shown in Table 1, we compare three settings to demonstrate the impact of our proposed Conditional Ground-Truth Injection (CGI): (1) No Injection (Standard GRPO) : Yields near-zero learning progress due to severe reward sparsity. (2) Unconditional Injection (S-GRPO wo CGI) : The ground-truth trajectory is injected into every single sampling group regardless of the policy’s success. As indicated by the experi-mental results, while this aggressive injection strategy helps solve the cold-start problem and can even further boost domain-specific performance, it inevitably compromises the model’s general capabilities. The constant presence of the ground truth introduces a strong "teacher-forcing" bias, severely limiting the model’s autonomous exploration. (3) Conditional Ground-Truth Injection (CGI - Ours) : By uti-lizing the binary verifier to detect group failures and triggering injection strictly as a fallback, CGI achieves the optimal bal-ance. It bootstraps the gradient during early cold-start phases to ensure domain-specific learning, and gracefully steps aside when unnecessary. This allows pure preference optimization to dominate, effectively protecting the model’s general capabilities while maintaining high domain performance. 

## 5.4 Parameter Study 

Impact of the Control Threshold ( 𝛿 ). The hyperparameter 𝛿 

regulates the strictness of the Conditional Ground-Truth Injection (CGI) mechanism. As shown in Table 2, increasing 𝛿 inherently raises the difficulty of achieving autonomous rewards, thereby trig-gering the ground-truth injection more frequently. This aggressive bootstrapping forces the model to exploit the provided trajectories, which substantially accelerates the acquisition of domain-specific knowledge (e.g., CIDEr improves dramatically from 0.2417 at 𝛿 = 0

to 1.0170 at 𝛿 = 1). However, this mechanism introduces a clear trade-off. An overly stringent threshold (e.g., 𝛿 = 1) induces a persistent "teacher-forcing" effect that suppresses the model’s au-tonomous exploratory capacity. Consequently, this over-reliance exerts a severe negative impact on the preservation of pre-trained general capabilities, as evidenced by the sharp degradation in MME (dropping to 2225.65) and IFEval-I (dropping to 0.6888). Our empir-ical results demonstrate that setting 𝛿 = 0.9 achieves the optimal Pareto balance: it yields near-peak domain-specific performance (1.0106 CIDEr) while robustly preserving the model’s structural integrity and general instruction-following abilities. 

## 5.5 Convergence & Learning Dynamics 

Demonstrating stability and computational efficiency, Figure 3 vi-sualizes S-GRPO’s learning dynamics. Driven by CGI, it exhibits a monotonically increasing reward curve culminating in rapid con-vergence. Providing high-quality anchor trajectories during the cold-start phase smooths the non-convex optimization landscape. This targeted injection breaks the symmetry of zero-reward sam-ples, guaranteeing directional policy gradients when vulnerable to reward sparsity. As capabilities improve, CGI dynamically scales back, enabling a seamless transition from supervised bootstrapping to pure preference optimization without gradient shocks. In stark contrast, standard GRPO lacks explicit trajectory guidance and fails to secure reliable advantage estimation under the strict binary verifier. Its learning curve suffers a severe "flatline" optimiza-tion collapse, remaining trapped in a zero-gradient plateau. While standard GRPO may occasionally exhibit sporadic reward spikes from rare exploratory successes, these noisy signals are overwhelm-ingly diluted by the vast majority of failed trajectories within the sampling group. Consequently, the contrastive advantage remains uninformative, rendering these fleeting successes insufficient to drive systemic policy updates. Ultimately, the standard RL paradigm wastes computational resources on unrewarded samples, fundamen-tally failing to accumulate the necessary gradients to assimilate the requisite domain-specific knowledge. ## 6 Conclusion 

We propose Supervised Group Relative Policy Optimization (S-GRPO), a novel unified framework that harmonizes domain knowl-edge injection and preference optimization into a single on-policy post-training stage. By introducing the Conditional Ground-Truth Injection (CGI) mechanism alongside a strict binary verifier, S-GRPO effectively resolves the optimization collapse and reward sparsity problems inherent in cold-start RL. Extensive experiments across diverse non-deep-thinking visual tasks demonstrate that S-GRPO achieves domain adaptation competitive with full fine-tuning, while significantly outperforming established two-stage pipelines in preserving the structural integrity and general-purpose capabilities of LVLMs. S-GRPO provides a robust, computationally elegant pathway for scaling specialized multimodal alignment.
