# SafeAnchor: Preventing Cumulative Safety Erosion in Continual Domain Adaptation of Large Language Models

Abstract 

Safety alignment in large language models is remarkably shallow: it is concentrated in the first few output tokens and reversible by fine-tuning on as few as 100 adversarial examples. This fragility becomes critical in real-world deployment, where models undergo sequential adaptation across domains such as medicine, law, and code, causing safety guardrails to erode cumulatively. Yet all existing safety-preserving methods target only single-task fine-tuning, leaving the multi-domain sequential setting entirely unaddressed. We introduce SafeAnchor , a framework that anchors safety in place throughout continual adaptation. SafeAnchor first identifies low-rank safety subspaces in LoRA parameter space via Fisher Information eigendecomposition, then constrains domain-specific gradient updates to the orthogonal complement of these subspaces, and finally monitors for residual safety drift with threshold-triggered corrective replay. Evaluated on Llama-2-7B-Chat and Mistral-7B-Instruct across a three-domain pipeline and eight benchmarks, SafeAnchor retains 93.2% of original safety alignment, outperforming all baselines by 18–42 points, while matching unconstrained fine-tuning to within 1.5 points on domain tasks. 

Keywords: AI Safety, Continual Learning, Large Language Models, Safety Alignment, LoRA, Domain Adaptation 

1. Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly adapted to specialized domains such as medicine, law, and code generation through continual fine-tuning on domain-specific data [1, 2]. In practice, a single model may undergo a sequence of adaptations as organizational needs evolve, forming a continual domain adaptation pipeline. While this sequential fine-tuning extends model capabilities, it poses a critical yet underexplored risk: the cumulative erosion of safety alignment. Recent work has established that safety alignment in LLMs is surprisingly brittle. Qi et al. [3] demonstrated that current alignment primarily modifies the generative distribution over the first few output tokens, teaching models to begin with refusal phrases rather than embedding deep behavioral changes. Ji et al. [4] introduced alignment elasticity , showing that LLMs inherently resist alignment and revert to pre-training behavior upon further fine-tuning, with this effect intensifying at larger scales. Even benign fine-tuning on standard datasets can compromise safety guardrails [5], and as few as 100 adversarial examples can undo 100,000 safety training instances [6]. The “safety tax” documented by Huang et al. [7] further reveals a fundamental trade-off between safety and reasoning capability. These findings reveal a fundamental vulnerability: each adaptation step risks degrading safety, and these degradations compound. Yet existing safety-preserving methods (Vaccine [8], RepNoise [9], Safe LoRA [10], SaLoRA [11], Lisa [12], SafeGrad [13]) all address only single-task adaptation. CL methods such as O-LoRA [14], InfLoRA [15], and GainLoRA [16] prevent task forgetting but ignore safety. Alssum et al. [17] and OGPSA [18] evaluate only single-task or the inverse problem. No existing work addresses safety during sequential multi-domain continual adaptation. We propose SafeAnchor , a framework that anchors safety alignment in place while enabling effective continual domain adaptation of LLMs. SafeAnchor integrates three components whose combination addresses the sequential safety-preservation setting (Figure 1):

(1) Safety Subspace Identification (SSI) uses gradient-based Fisher Information analysis [19–21] to identify LoRA parameter directions encoding safety, updated incrementally after each domain via explicit subspace merging. 

(2) Orthogonal Safety-Constrained Adaptation (OSCA) projects LoRA gradient updates onto the orthogonal complement of the safety subspace, extending gradient projection from single-task safety [13] and continual learning [15, 22] to the sequential safety-preservation setting. 

(3) Cumulative Safety Monitoring (CSM) evaluates safety on a held-out probe set after each domain using LlamaGuard [23], triggering corrective replay when degradation exceeds a threshold. > 2.1. Fragility of Safety Alignment

Standard alignment techniques produce safety behaviors that are surprisingly shallow. Qi et al. [3] showed alignment concentrates in the first few output tokens; Ji et al. [4] demonstrated that LLMs exhibit alignment elasticity , reverting to pre-training behavior upon further fine-tuning, with this effect worsening at scale. Even benign fine-tuning compromises safety [5], and as few as 100 adversarial examples can undo extensive safety training [6]. The safety tax [7] further documents a fundamental safety–capability trade-off. Together, these findings motivate our work: if single-step fine-tuning already degrades safety, sequential multi-domain adaptation compounds the problem.  

> 2.2. Safety-Preserving Fine-Tuning

Existing defenses operate at three stages [34]. Alignment-stage methods modify the base model before fine-tuning: Vaccine [8] produces perturbation-invariant embeddings, and RepNoise [9] removes harmful representations. Fine-tuning-stage methods constrain the adaptation process: Lisa [12] alternates alignment and task optimization, and SafeGrad [13] projects away safety-conflicting gradients. Post-fine-tuning methods adjust adapted weights: Safe LoRA [10] projects LoRA onto safety-aligned subspaces, and SaLoRA [11] orthogonalizes against safety features. Despite this rich landscape, every method addresses only single-task adaptation , leaving cumulative erosion across sequential domains unaddressed.

While OSCA prevents direct interference with the safety subspace, indirect effects (such as changes to shared representations that affect safety through non-linear pathways) may still accumulate across domain transitions. CSM addresses this through a lightweight monitoring and correction mechanism. 

Safety Probe. We maintain a held-out safety probe set Dprobe (disjoint from Dsafe , 200 examples from HarmBench [29]). After each domain adaptation t, we evaluate the safety refusal rate st on Dprobe using LlamaGuard [23] as the binary safety classifier (which achieves 92.1% F1 on our probe set for distinguishing safe refusals from harmful completions). 

Threshold-Triggered Safety Replay. If st < (1 − τ ) s0 (where s0 is the baseline refusal rate and τ is the fractional tolerance), CSM triggers a brief safety replay: we fine-tune for 

Erepair = 200 steps on a mixture of the safety calibration set and the current domain data, using the OSCA-projected gradients, to restore the safety score. The value Erepair = 200 sits at the knee of a diminishing-returns curve (Appendix C): 100 steps recover only +1 .9 points vs. +2 .7 at 200 , while 400 adds a further +0 .2 points at twice the cost plus 0.4 points of domain regression. After replay, we re-evaluate st to verify st ≥ (1 − τ ) s0; if not, replay is extended by at most one further Erepair block. The replay objective is:

4.2. Main Results 

Tables 1 and 2 present our main results. On Llama-2-7B-Chat, standard LoRA causes a catastrophic 47 .8-point safety drop. SafeGrad+LoRA achieves the best single-method baseline ( 67 .4 ± 1.4) but still loses 24 points. Safety Interleaving ( 64 .8 ± 1.6) confirms passive data mixing is insufficient. SafeAnchor retains 85 .2 ± 0.9 (93.2% of original safety ), with domain performance within 1.3 points of unconstrained LoRA. The pattern holds on Mistral (93.1% safety, 1.5-point gap). CL methods (O-LoRA, EWC) perform worse than safety-specific methods, confirming targeted preservation is necessary.

Safety Subspace Validation. To validate the low-rank safety subspace assumption (Section 3.2), we examined the eigenvalue spectrum of the Fisher Information matrix on the safety calibration set. Across all LoRA layers, the spectrum decays sharply: on average 

∼8 eigenvectors capture 90% of the total variance, while a random subset of training data produces a near-flat spectrum. This confirms that safety information in LoRA parameter space is genuinely low-rank, consistent with activation-space findings [19, 21]. We further analyze the stability of this subspace across sequential domain adaptations (principal-angle and Grassmannian-distance analyses) in Appendix D. 

4.5. Sensitivity Analysis 

We examine sensitivity to key hyperparameters (Table 4). Increasing ρ from 0.80 to 0.95 improves safety (+3.1) at moderate domain cost ( −2.2). CSM threshold τ controls monitoring sensitivity. SafeAnchor is also robust to calibration size: even Ns = 100 achieves 83.1 safety, which is above all baselines, confirming stable Fisher eigenvector estimation. 5. Conclusion 

We introduced SafeAnchor, a framework for preserving safety alignment during continual domain adaptation of LLMs. By combining Safety Subspace Identification, Orthogonal Safety-Constrained Adaptation, and Cumulative Safety Monitoring, SafeAnchor retains over 93% of original safety after three sequential adaptations with competitive domain performance. While individual components draw on established techniques, their principled integration for sequential safety preservation is validated across two architectures, eight benchmarks, and full variance reporting. 

Limitations and Future Work. Our evaluation is limited to 7B-scale models and a primary focus of three sequential domains; Appendix A extends to T = 5 and finds SafeAnchor’s near-linear trajectory continues (slope ≈1.9 pts/step), but rigorous study at 

T ≥5 across orderings remains open. Alignment elasticity worsens with scale [4], making 13B+ extension important; we expect SafeAnchor’s Fisher-subspace machinery to transfer, but ks and Fisher-trace distributions may shift. Longer sequences could eventually exhaust the orthogonal complement (manifesting as subspace inflation or gradient cancellation); neither is observed at T =5 (App. D). Future work includes integration with alignment-stage defenses [8, 44] and non-LoRA methods [45]. Reproducibility. All materials are available at: https://github.com/bettyguo/ SafeAnchor .
