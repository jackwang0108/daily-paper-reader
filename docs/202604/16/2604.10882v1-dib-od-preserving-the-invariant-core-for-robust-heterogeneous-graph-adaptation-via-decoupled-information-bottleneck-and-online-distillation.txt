# Abstract 

Graph Neural Network pretraining is pivotal for leveraging unlabeled graph data. However, gen-eralizing across heterogeneous domains remains a major challenge due to severe distribution shifts. Existing methods primarily focus on intra-domain patterns, failing to disentangle task-relevant in-variant knowledge from domain-specific redundant noise, leading to negative transfer and catastrophic forgetting. To this end, we propose DIB-OD , a novel framework designed to preserve the invari-ant core for robust heterogeneous graph adapta-tion through a Decoupled Information Bottleneck and Online Distillation framework. Our core in-novation is the explicit decomposition of repre-sentations into orthogonal invariant and redundant subspaces. By utilizing an Information Bottle-neck teacher-student distillation mechanism and the Hilbert-Schmidt Independence Criterion, we isolate a stable invariant core that transcends do-main boundaries. Furthermore, a self-adaptive semantic regularizer is introduced to protect this core from corruption during target-domain adapta-tion by dynamically gating label influence based on predictive confidence. Extensive experiments across chemical, biological, and social network do-mains demonstrate that DIB-OD significantly out-performs state-of-the-art methods, particularly in challenging inter-type domain transfers, showcas-ing superior generalization and anti-forgetting per-formance. 

# 1 Introduction 

Graph Neural Networks have revolutionized representation learning across diverse fields, from molecular property pre-diction to social network analysis. To mitigate the scarcity of labeled data, the ”pre-train then fine-tune” paradigm has emerged, aiming to capture transferable structural patterns from vast unlabeled graph repositories. This pursuit of cross-domain generalization is also a fundamental challenge in

many AI fields. Despite its success, a critical generaliza-tion wall persists: most existing GNN pre-training and do-main adaptation frameworks are designed for intra-domain shifts—addressing distribution variations within the same category of data (e.g., regional shifts in citation networks or sensor noise in protein graphs). Recently, an emerging trend has seen the development of universal graph models that aim to aggregate knowl-edge across disparate data sources [Zhao et al. , 2025; He et al. , 2025; Fang et al. , 2025]. While these pioneer efforts have demonstrated the potential of large-scale graph learn-ing, a critical representation bottleneck remains largely un-addressed across the field. Existing pre-training strategies, including those emerging universal frameworks, often treat latent features as monolithic entities. This results in a funda-mental information entanglement: domain-specific structural styles are deeply entangled with the task-relevant topological laws. In heterogeneous transfer scenarios, the noise’s repre-sentation from a source domain often acts as a spurious cor-relation that misleads the model when it encounters a target domain with a completely different connectivity logic, lead-ing to negative transfer. Furthermore, a significant hurdle for pre-trained GNNs is their vulnerability during target-domain adaptation. When a model that has captured universal structural principles is fine-tuned on a new domain, it risks the deficiency of catas-trophic forgetting. Without an explicit mechanism to isolate and protect the transferable core, the target-domain-specific signals often contaminate and overwrite the hard-won gener-alizable knowledge. This tension between transferability and specificity undermines the very purpose of cross-domain pre-training. To address these limitations, we propose DIB-OD, a uni-versal pre-training framework based on Decoupled Infor-mation Bottleneck and Online Distillation. Our approach shifts the focus from mere ”data aggregation” to ”knowledge purification.” Built upon a multi-view distillation architec-ture, DIB-OD explicitly decomposes the learned representa-tion into two orthogonal components: (1) a domain-invariant core representation that captures universal, transferable graph principles, and (2) a domain-specific redundant representa-tion that absorbs category-specific noise. Guided by the In-formation Bottleneck principle and enforced by the Hilbert-Schmidt Independence Criterion [Gretton et al. , 2007] (HSIC

Un-like fixed constraints, this regularizer dynamically modulates the influence of target labels based on predictive confidence, effectively shielding the invariant core from being overwrit-ten by noisy target signals. • A Purification Paradigm for Heterogeneous Trans-fer: We identify the ”information entanglement” issue in universal graph learning and propose a novel decoupling framework that bridges fundamentally different graph categories. • Decoupled Information Bottleneck Distillation: We introduce an optimization scheme that utilizes IB-guided distillation and HSIC-based orthogonality to isolate in-variant graphical structures, ensuring its purity and transferability. • Self-adaptive Knowledge Preservation: We design a confidence-based adaptive regularizer and a “frozen teacher” strategy to protect universal knowledge dur-ing adaptation, effectively mitigating catastrophic for-getting. • Extensive Benchmarking: Extensive experiments on several cross-domain benchmarks demonstrate that DIB-OD significantly outperforms state-of-the-art meth-ods, especially in challenging inter-type domain trans-fers (e.g., Chem → Bio), showcasing superior general-ization. 

# 2 Related Works 

2.1 From Intra-domain Adaptation to Heterogeneous Generalization 

Graph Domain Adaptation mitigates distribution shifts, con-ventionally focusing on intra-domain scenarios where pat-terns vary but categories remain consistent [You et al. , 2023; Fang et al. , 2025]. However, these methods rely on structural overlap, proving ineffective for heterogeneous cross-domain pre-training where disjoint feature spaces create a “general-ization wall.” To break this barrier, recent research pivots to-ward Out-of-Distribution (OOD) generalization and invariant learning. Works such as [Li et al. , 2022; Chen et al. , 2023] attempt to discover stable substructures invariant to environ-mental changes. Recent domain generalization works like GraphAny [Zhao et al. , 2025] and UniGraph [He et al. , 2025] address this by capturing universal inductive biases or align-ing disparate datasets into a unified space. Despite this, most frameworks treat representations as monolithic entities, prior-itizing global alignment over internal disentanglement. This necessitates a shift from data aggregation to explicit knowl-edge purification, motivating our proposal to mathematically decouple the invariant core from redundant noise. 

2.2 Information-Theoretic Disentangled Representation 

Multiview Disentangled Representation Learning separates shared consistency from view-specific information using the Information Bottleneck principle [Yin et al. , 2024; Fan et al. ,2023] or adversarial constraints [Wang et al. , 2024]. Exist-ing frameworks, however, are largely confined to in-domain datasets. In heterogeneous settings, the failure to separate transferable principles from domain-specific noise ( e.g. con-nectivity styles) leads to negative transfer. DIB-OD addresses this by extending the Information Bottleneck principle to het-erogeneous pre-training, explicitly enforcing orthogonality between invariant and redundant subspaces to ensure only “universal graph laws” are transferred. 

2.3 Knowledge Decomposition via Online Distillation 

While Knowledge Distillation typically involves student-teacher imitation [Park et al. , 2019; Chen et al. , 2021], ex-isting graph KD methods [Joshi et al. , 2022] lack mecha-nisms to distinguish the generalizable core from redundant noise. This “imitation-based” approach leaves models vulner-able to catastrophic forgetting, as fine-tuning on new domains can overwrite pre-trained structural wisdom. Unlike these paradigms, our DIB-OD utilizes online distillation to decon-struct knowledge components, employing a Self-adaptive Se-mantic Regularizer to shield the invariant core from corrup-tion by target-specific noise.

4.1 Experimental Settings 

Dataset. To evaluate our model performance, we per-form experiments on several well-known datasets [Mor-ris et al. , 2020], includes NCI109, FRANKENSTEIN, deezer ego nets, Mutagenicity, PROTEINS, IMDB-BINARY and D&D . Among them, NCI109, FRANKENSTEIN, and Mutagenicity are chemical molecules graph datasets. PRO-TEINS and D&D are graph datasets from the bioinformatics domain. IMDB-BINARY and deezer ego nets fall into the category of social networks dataset. The detailed statics are list in Appendix B. 

Baseline Models. 

To situate our work within the existing literature, we com-pare against key representative methods. This includes foun-dational GNNs like GAT [Veliˇ ckovi´ c et al. , 2017], domain-adversarial approach DANN [Ganin et al. , 2016], and GDA-specific models DANE [Zhang et al. , 2019], UDAGCN [Wu

These baseline methods address vari-ous aspects of graph structure, attribute, or global distribution alignment, yet none explicitly investigate the impact of ho-mophily distribution shifts on graph domain adaptation per-formance. 

Default settings. We evaluate our framework on down-stream graph classification tasks, adopting Accuracy as the primary evaluation metric. During the pre-training phase, we employ a 10-fold cross-validation strategy to optimize both the teacher and student models. For cross-domain fine-tuning, we split the labeled data using 10-fold cross-validation. The detailed graph augmentation parameters are listed in Appendix C. The GNN backbone consists of a three-layer Graph Con-volutional Network, which is followed by an MLP-based lo-gistic regression layer for the final classification. Adhering to the online distillation paradigm, we simultaneously update the parameters of both the teacher and student models dur-ing pre-training. Subsequently, during the fine-tuning phase, the teacher model’s parameters are frozen, and only the stu-dent model is trained. For both pre-training and fine-tuning, all models are trained for 100 epochs. We employ the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.001 for all experiments. Besides, for the DIB-OD framework, we will provide more specific hyperparameter configurations. In the teacher’s IB loss ( LIBT ), the trade-off parameter βT is set to 0.1. For the student’s IB objective ( LIBS ), both βy and βvs are set to 0.05 .The weight for the HSIC orthogonality constraint, λOrth , is also set to 0.05 . In the final objective function (Eq. (14)), the scaling factor for the total information bottleneck term, λIB ,is 0.01 . Finally, the weights for the reconstruction loss, λR,and the knowledge distillation loss, λKD , are set to 0.001 and 

0.01 , respectively.

To investigate the individual contribution of each compo-nent in DIB-OD, we conduct a comprehensive ablation study across both intra-type and inter-type graph transfer tasks. The results are summarized in Table 3. 

Efficacy of SSR: The removal of the SSR (setting κ = 1 )leads to a significant performance decline, particularly in challenging transfers like F → N (from 69.83% to 52.10%) and M → N (from 68.50% to 51.44%). This drop sug-gests that without class-aware adaptive weighting, the model treats all structural knowledge equally, allowing noisy target-specific signals to overwrite the hard-won ”invariant core.” The SSR’s ability to protect high-confidence semantic knowl-edge is crucial for maintaining the stability of representations during adaptation. 

Mitigating Catastrophic Forgetting: The comparison with Full Fine-tuning where the teacher model is not frozen highlights the risk of knowledge corruption. In scenarios such as N → F , full fine-tuning results in lower accuracy (56.42%) compared to DIB-OD (57.30%). This confirms that keep-ing a stable ”frozen teacher” as a knowledge anchor prevents the model from over-adjusting to the target domain’s distri-bution, thereby preserving the generalizable graph principles captured during pre-training. 

The Necessity of Information Compression: By setting 

βT = 0 and βvs (w/o IB), we observe a drastic performance collapse in heterogeneous transfers, such as M → N. The compression terms I(Zϕ; XΦ) and I(zvs ; zvr ) serve as filters that discard domain-specific ”redundant styles. Without this bottleneck, the student model’s zvs becomes contaminated with spurious correlations from the source domain, leading to severe negative transfer when the model encounters the dif-ferent connectivity logic of the target domain. 

The Role of Orthogonal Decoupling: The results for re-moving HSIC further validate our decoupling strategy. In tasks like P → N and N → P , the absence of the orthogonality constraint in Equation (8)) leads to a 1.0% to 2.0% lead reduc-tion. This empirical evidence supports our theoretical claim: while IB guides the information flow, the HSIC-based regu-larizer ensures that the invariant and redundant subspaces are statistically independent. Without this forced separation, the invariant core fails to isolate itself from category-dependent noise, which limits the model’s ability to bridge fundamen-tally different graph categories.
